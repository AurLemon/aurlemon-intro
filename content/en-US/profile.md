## Junior college · Phase 1

I'll open a separate section to talk about how I ended up in secondary/vocational and junior college (I mix "higher vocational" and "junior college" here; same idea).

Because more than one person asked or chatted about this →_→

I missed the cutoff for general high school in the high school entrance exam and slipped to the second batch choice, which was a five-year higher-vocational program, so I ended up there. Even if I hadn't slipped, I could only have barely gotten into a private school in the urban area. Looking back, failing the exam and going to secondary vocational (3+2 secondary stage) was a rare heavyweight moment; basically I just messed around for three years and learned nothing.

99% my own problem. Looking back I'm even more ashamed of letting down my junior high head teacher and physics teacher, and the teachers who taught me Σ*(꒪ཀ꒪」∠)* Back then in junior high I wasn't exactly a nobody, and I even pulled off a big stunt for a certain teacher (lol).

After I got to secondary vocational school, everyone kept comforting each other that our five-year program not only **beat the city general high school line**, but had the **second-highest** cutoff among the same batch. But I knew where I really stood (┯_┯).

In secondary vocational school, because our cutoff was the highest in the school, the attention was also the highest. I couldn't accept it and did some absurd things (like wanting to drop out and take the normal exam; luckily a teacher stopped me). Not long after arriving, because I had played with computers a lot and built a few sites, I suddenly became the center. Within a few months I passed the Level 1 and Level 2 exams. I really didn't expect that; in junior high I was mid-to-low in the grade, and suddenly I became top.

Later in secondary vocational school I just took it easy. The pressure was low and I was really idle, so I tinkered with small stuff bit by bit, and sometimes thought about why things work. The study intensity was still much lower than junior high.

Then I got to junior college (Chuanzheng). Our 3+2 students only study two years there, so we aren't taken seriously. And because the high school stage was missing, I struggled a bit at the university stage. Plus the school's messy stuff is real, including but not limited to all kinds of mandatory activities and student bureaucracy. My junior college story isn't over yet, so I'll stop here for now ¥ㄟ(´･ᴗ･`)ノ¥

If you are interested in my earlier stories, you can DM me. There are too many stories, so the details here are vague. I especially welcome people with similar experiences or who are interested in my story, or who want to connect with fellow nerds (^・ω・^)

Looking back, it's been almost five years. There are still a few days in a week when I think about junior high and the faces and advice of the teachers I disappointed. Still very ashamed.

After the entrance exam, I watched classmates with similar or even lower scores go abroad or to boarding schools. It was the first time I felt the gap between people. In the future, I **must** keep studying (top-up degree). Part of it is unwillingness, but more is wanting a restart. The feeling of letting people down is, looking back, one of the most painful things.

It feels like Git: the main branch has problems, you can't open a new branch or roll back, but with continuous commits, you can still get back on track.

## Junior college · Phase 2

(The paragraph above was written in winter 2025; back then I probably didn't expect the second half of the year to go beyond imagination.)

At the start of the second semester of my first year, on the second day of school, my course teacher X asked me: **Want to join a competition? Nationals!** My brain blanked out.

I thought for a class (I had contacted a project team before but felt too much pressure from seniors, so I didn't go; plus I had just started first year and wasn't used to it). In the end I decided to push my comfort zone, and told the teacher: **OK.** So I went to the competition training room.

Then I went in and met seniors and another lead teacher (the lead teacher was the main one; the teacher who pulled me in was the assistant). Call him H? Anyway, then I applied for training leave - no need to deal with campus running or finals.

Then H brought me into a project in the meteorology field. I did frontend. I slacked for half a year and then bad news: the project died. That was it. Oh, during that time I also passed CET-4 with 516, a bit regretful; hope the year-end CET-6 also works and can pass 500.

The other thing was the national competition. Full name: World Vocational College Skills Competition Finals (New-generation IT track). I didn't expect the chance to come to me. H teacher recruited two more newcomers besides me, and I was recruited by X. But those two later also did projects; one ran away and the other couldn't learn and went inactive for a while.

So we prepared for the competition. It's not a pure tech competition; it's more like pitching and performance, similar to innovation/entrepreneurship contests. Our teachers stitched together three enterprise projects they had done, and it became a new project.

So the project existed, but we lacked the script and performance. We four procrastinated writing the script until 14 days before the contest. Why 14 days? Because the nationals merged many tracks, and our college had two teams, one of them was ours, so the leaders started putting pressure on us. They wanted to see how our two teams performed (late August). The result: no teamwork. Everyone's script was separate, with no connection or dialogue. Then we started rewriting.

After rewriting, we did a second tryout (Sept 1). This time it blew up completely. The script was not smooth; each part was just simple AI; no one watched their own part carefully. We froze, we were cold, we didn't know what to say. The teachers below didn't understand. We barely finished and got criticized. Sept 1 was the day our pressure peaked.

Then revise! Keep revising! We argued for a long time, and I eventually went up and rewrote the script heavily. In the next days we rehearsed like crazy, with slight tweaks. (Haha, we worked until 11-12 every day, but H teacher drove me home - a perk I never thought I'd have.) We had to leave on Sept 6 and compete on the 9th-10th. If the script still wasn't smooth, we were doomed. I was anxious every day.

On the 6th, we arrived at the hotel and kept practicing. After devices were sealed, we kept practicing. The three days before the competition were the hardest. After drawing lots and confirming we would compete the next day (the 10th), it got worse. We also learned another team in our college won gold! More pressure. We originally thought a bronze would be fine, but now bronze was the minimum requirement.

On competition night I couldn't sleep. Fell asleep at 2:30, had to get up at 5:30. Then on the bus, into the arena. After entering, there was a second draw; we got third from last, C09 (Group C, order 09). We entered the waiting room at 7am; eleven groups total, so we wouldn't start until 3-4pm. We just sat. Two outgoing seniors couldn't take it and went to ask the group behind us; we learned they were from Heilongjiang. Then both sides exchanged info and pressure (lol, fun).

One hour before going on stage I started panicking. I kept walking in circles in the waiting room. We were told to collect devices, then entered and debugged. Then we waited in a small room next to the competition classroom. The last ten minutes were the hardest. The volunteer who brought us in was resting nearby; he saw me pacing and laughed, and a senior told the volunteers: "Ever seen someone nervous? Not this nervous."

Then we entered! I actually got sleepy because I only slept three hours and started forgetting lines. In the first Q&A I mumbled and messed up; felt like the sky fell. I recovered later, but still had a few stumbles in the performance. The judges looked calm, probably tired after so many teams. The technical demo was fine.

After the performance, the score reveal was a surprise. Those volunteers even peeked at our competition, lol, kind of cute. After we came out, they asked how it went. We couldn't hide our smiles, but I held back my excitement and said "It was okay!" The volunteer led us out and asked where we were from. I jokingly said "across from Taiwan". The volunteer at the back said "...Hainan?" "Close enough."

Then the vice dean and teachers treated us to dinner, and booked tickets to Nanjing Museum; we went to Nanjing the next day. On the way we played and kept worrying: silver? silver? gold? gold?!

But it was my second time in Nanjing; honestly nothing special. The most memorable part was the teacher renting a car, driving us around, and the fun stories. Then halfway through Qinhuai River, we heard the results. The list came out and my heart sank: fourth place. Three points short of silver. First was 649, no complaint. Second 620, third 619, and we were 616?!

Then the regret. On the closing day, we took a group photo with the Heilongjiang team we chatted with. After the closing ceremony, the teachers drove us around again and we went to Tianmuhu.

Then we went back. Not writing all the details, but the process was unforgettable, my first time. Even if I compete again, it will be alone. Teacher driving me home, teacher driving the team around and getting a ticket from traffic police, teachers and dean teasing each other, "bullying" a teammate in the hotel (actually correcting posture, but three people surrounding one looked like bullying haha), and gossip about teammates... From the pressure and anxiety at the start, to excitement and joy, to regret and acceptance. This roller-coaster experience is rare. Felt like a dream.

I didn't expect to win anything. Silver would have been nice, but bronze is enough for the top-up exam award. Even though our province uses a weighted system, it's still enough. Public universities are easy to get in, right? For top-up it's basically only second-tier anyway, nothing to pick. But it still feels unreal. Right now the biggest worry is where to get my internship stamp.
