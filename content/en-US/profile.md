## Education

1. Middle school: **Fuzhou Pingdong Middle School** (2018 - 2021).
2. After the high school entrance exam, I was admitted to the 3+2 **five-year vocational program** in Computer Application Technology under Fujian Chuanzheng Communications College. My upper-secondary and junior-college years were spent at **Fujian Engineering & Economics School** (2021 - 2024) and **Fujian Chuanzheng Communications College** (2024 - 2026).
3. After transferring to the undergraduate track, I was admitted to **Fujian University of Technology** for Artificial Intelligence (2026 - 2028).
4. Hopefully there is an "after that" too (2028 - 2031).

Why was junior college only two years? Because I came from the five-year track that starts after the middle school entrance exam.

As for why I ended up there, I will explain that later. First, let me talk about the transfer-to-undergraduate major choice and the decisions around it. How long did I prepare? In September 2025 I joined the World Vocational College Skills Competition with a teacher, so I was eligible to apply through the award-recipient route. Because of that, my actual exam preparation started rather late, only in January 2026. The final score was still good enough, and I chose the school I wanted through the early admission route. Why did I choose Artificial Intelligence as the major? Just because Fujian University of Technology is in Fuzhou, close to home. When I choose schools, comfort and convenience come first. As for the major itself, electronic information or computer-related fields are both acceptable to me.

During the application period, a lot of friends also advised me not to apply for Artificial Intelligence. Their argument was basically that "AI is hard to find work in unless you are from a 985 or 211 school," and Fujian University of Technology is, after all, just a non-Double-First-Class first-tier university. That claim is not completely detached from reality, but it is still far too rough. Employment is never determined by the name of a major alone. Choosing AI as a major does not mean I must work in AI in the future either, assuming I do go straight into employment after graduating from the undergraduate program.

From the standpoint of school choice convenience, the major was only secondary for me. Artificial Intelligence is more or less harmless in that regard. To be a bit shameless, I think of myself as a CS Student anyway. I even wrote that in my GitHub bio. Still, I also want to say something about a certain type of person who keeps spreading anxiety by repeating things like "you will not find work" or "how could someone from a non-Double-First-Class transfer track possibly find work." Deconstructing everything endlessly only leads to nihilism. Pointing out a problem does not mean you can solve it or avoid it. At that point it becomes a turn-based, never-ending cycle of internal exhaustion.

I am not denying degree filters or employment pressure. It is not that I refuse to accept the possibility of unemployment, and I definitely do not think I am so capable that I am guaranteed to find a job. I am impatient too. What I dislike is the habit of repeating real-world difficulty as if it were destiny. Pointing out a problem is not the same thing as solving it, and repeatedly emphasizing the probability of failure does not bring anyone closer to an answer. If you keep deconstructing everything, what remains is just nihilism. Endless nihilism and cynicism.

## Why Junior College

Now let me answer the question, "How did you end up in junior college?" More precisely, the real question should be, "How did you not get into high school?" Why did that happen? I took the Fuzhou entrance exam in 2021. My score passed the general high-school cutoff, but not enough for a good school. On top of that, I did not take the application process seriously, so I went straight into the five-year vocational path. Looking back, I am actually glad I ended up where I am now. I do not know whether I could have reached the same place if I had gone to a regular high school. Going from the five-year vocational track to a transfer-to-undergraduate path, I think I was lucky.

This was a kind of alternate route. The cost, however, is that HR can filter out transfer-to-undergraduate candidates with a single click. You could say I was naive in middle school and only cared about having fun. But that explanation is not convincing enough. Poor scores and bad outcomes were only surface-level symptoms. I have asked myself before: what was my middle-school life actually like? Everything was pushing me forward, yet I had no idea where I was supposed to go. Back then, my main anxiety was never "How should I learn?" but "What if I simply cannot learn this?"

At that time, I would not even try. Why did I not try? Why did I not force myself to push through? If I had done that then, many of the problems I face now should have been solved back then. Why did it drag on for so long? Still, I was also very helpless at the time. No one really helped me. No one guided me. I did not even know how to ask questions. I could only rely on myself. From 2021 to 2024, reality was like a massive test set, while my generalization ability was terrible. The end result was constant overfitting and underfitting. I lacked a feedback loop, and I also lacked genuinely useful methods to help me get out of that situation.

I went through countless anxious nights and deceived myself with all kinds of baseless "patterns." That period was ordinary on the surface, but deeply depressing. What was I supposed to do? Xiaohongshu was full of study methods. Bilibili was full of tutorials. A relative smiling and asking, "So which vocational school are you planning to attend?" and a close friend saying, "Your English is good, you have to work hard later, you still have a chance" followed by "That is just the truth, vocational school is vocational school" both pulled me right back into reality. When other people were using family resources to attend schools elsewhere or study abroad, by the time I understood what was happening and looked back, it already felt too late. Anxiety and impatience were the main themes of that period.

My middle school was a high-standard reference frame for me, full of excellent people. When the word "junior college," a term that had been repeatedly mocked in that environment, appeared on my school record for the first time, "absurd" was the word that best described how I felt. But I could not change it. I did not have the ability to achieve academic excellence, yet I had somehow developed the disease of worshipping it.

Back then I also lacked any real reference frame. I mistakenly treated the small circles I lived in online as if they were the whole world. I confused a local optimum for a global optimum. I was blindly arrogant. I would learn a few computing concepts and become pleased with myself. I touched computers early, I knew how to build sites, I could edit videos, I could make good-looking PPTs. It all looked versatile, but was any of it actually useful? No. Because aside from those things, I could no longer build even a basic sense of security for myself. Even so, I am still glad I got exposed to computing at all. At least I locked in my career direction early enough, and had enough spare time to continue figuring it out.

Wait, this is starting to sound too miserable. Why did I end up in the five-year vocational track? Because I was not good enough. Why did I choose computing there? Because this so-called "computer ability" was one of the very few things I had that stood out even a little. I only knew how to play with computers and mess around. What these five years have taught me is that standing still, or endlessly deconstructing things, or internally consuming yourself, or even spreading hidden anxiety to others, is completely useless. In the worst case, it becomes structural cynicism and can even drag other people down with it. That is extremely irresponsible.

The biggest lesson of these five years is that the first step to accomplishing anything is to start doing it. Instead of counting on other people, or on some so-called God, it is better to look at how much you can do right now.
